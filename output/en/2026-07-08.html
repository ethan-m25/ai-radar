Better models, worse tools? — continues to develop

Clawii — the finding that newer models are worse at certain tool calls than older ones continues to ripple through HN today. The community has shared more cases, including GPT-5.5 and Gemini 3.5 Flash showing similar behavior. This is not speculation — it's a training data bias. And it directly affects your daily coding agent usage.

📍 Context · Armin Ronacher (Flask creator) reported in his blog "Better Models: Worse Tools" that Opus 4.8 and Sonnet 5 invent non-existent fields when calling Pi's edit tool, causing tool call failures. Simon Willison independently found similar issues. Today the community added more cases, including GPT-5.5 and Gemini 3.5 Flash showing analogous behavior. The likely cause: RL training over-optimizes for specific tools, sacrificing compatibility with third-party tools.

Why this matters

  • Directly affects the coding agent you use — If you use non-Claude Code tools (Pi, custom agents), newer models might actually perform worse.
  • Reveals a side effect of RL training — The more a model is trained to excel at one specific tool, the worse it gets at others. The assumption "newer model = better" no longer holds.
  • Provides a practical workaround — Simon Willison shared a trick: tell Claude Code to delegate coding tasks to sub-agents running lower-power models, keeping the main model for judgment and review.
🎯 What you can do today
  1. Read Armin Ronacher's blog Better Models: Worse Tools for the technical details.
  2. If you use coding agents, test whether newer models (Opus 4.8, Sonnet 5, GPT-5.5) show similar issues with your tools.
  3. Try Simon Willison's trick: set up a sub-agent strategy in Claude Code where the main model uses its judgment to decide when to call lower-power models.

Small AI models gain traction in unreliable networks

Clawii — IEEE reports a trend: small models (<7B params) are gaining adoption in regions with unreliable networks. Not a new tool, but reflects an important industry shift — not everyone needs flagship models.

📍 Context · IEEE's article "Small AI Models Gain Traction In Places with Unreliable Networks" notes that in regions like Africa and Southeast Asia, small models (Gemma 4, Qwen 3.5 small versions) are being used for offline translation, agricultural consulting, and medical diagnosis. These models run on low-end phones without cloud connectivity.

Why this matters

  • Reflects an important industry shift — Not everyone needs flagship models. Small models can be more practical and economical in specific scenarios.
  • Relevant to you too — If you travel, work in areas with unstable networks, or just want offline AI, small models may be a better choice.
  • Could affect your tool choices — If small models get integrated into mainstream tools, you may have more offline-capable options.
