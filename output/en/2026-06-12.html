Simon Willison's deep-dive: Claude Fable "relentlessly proactive" — almost too proactive

Yesterday's radar covered the Fable 5 silent-intervention rollback, but what you should actually spend your weekend on is Simon Willison's 2,400-word hands-on report published today. His verdict: Fable 5 is "relentlessly proactive" — it knows what you're about to do next and just does it, even before you ask. That superpower is also its biggest pitfall.

📍 Context

Simon Willison is a well-known figure in the Python community and the author of the llm CLI tool. He was one of the first non-Anthropic people to get Fable 5 access. Over the past two days he put it through its paces: rewriting llm's ask_user feature, building RAG prototypes, and running prompt injection tests. His report is the most detailed third-party review so far. Fable 5 is priced at $10/M input tokens and $50/M output tokens — substantially more expensive than Opus 4.8.

Why this matters

  • "Proactivity" is Fable 5's core differentiator — Simon says it feels nothing like Opus 4.8: Fable 5 proactively suggests optimizations, calls tools, creates new files, and acts without waiting for instructions. Like an overeager intern who's already done the research before you finish asking.
  • Price is the real wall — Simon estimates his 2,400-word blog post (including all experiments) cost $150 in API fees. He jokes "good thing Anthropic gave me free credits, otherwise I wouldn't use it like this." For self-funded users, Fable 5 costs 5–10x more than Opus 4.8.
  • Concrete reference for vibe coders — Simon used Fable 5 to rewrite llm's ask_user feature (allowing the AI to ask the user questions mid-execution) in just 90 minutes. His take: "If this is the price of future work, worth it. But right now it's too expensive."
  • HN heat is sustained — Today "Claude Fable is relentlessly proactive" sits at #11 (477 pts), "Anthropic apologizes for invisible guardrails" at #12 (433 pts), and the open-sourced MiMo Code at #13 (504 pts). Fable 5 remains the community's focus.

Why is Fable 5 more proactive? — Simon notes Fable 5 has "proactivity" baked into its system prompt. It doesn't just answer — it suggests better implementations, auto-runs code to check for errors, fixes bugs without being asked, and proposes next steps after completing a task. This is incredibly powerful for fast iteration but can be problematic when you need precise, controlled output.

What Fable 5 is good for: Simon found it excels at 1) complex multi-toolchain tasks, 2) rapid prototyping, and 3) open-ended "inspiration" problems. Not good for: 1) cost-sensitive batch processing, 2) tasks requiring highly reproducible output, or 3) beginners (the over-proactivity can be confusing).

Impact on you as a vibe-coding comp manager: Use Fable 5 for "creative breakout" tasks — building an automation script from scratch, wrangling a complex Excel pipeline, or exploratory analysis. For day-to-day office work (emails, reports), Opus 4.8 is more than enough and way cheaper. Best strategy: Fable 5 as your "inspiration engine," Opus 4.8 as your "execution engine."

Honest caveats: 1) Costs are absurdly high (Simon: "it limits what I'm willing to do with it"); 2) Over-proactivity creates security risk (easier to exploit via prompt injection); 3) The model burns tokens on non-core tasks (auto-searching, analyzing codebase structure) driving costs up further; 4) No public third-party benchmark numbers — only Anthropic's own.
