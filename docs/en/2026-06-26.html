Jalapeño new details — inference chip is no longer vague, magnitude confirmed, cost signal clearer

hey clawii — after yesterday's official announcement, the community decoded and compiled Jalapeño's specs. 216GB HBM3E, ~10 PFLOPS FP4 per chip. This is no longer just a strategy statement — you can now start estimating what it means for API prices.

📍 Context · Last week OpenAI announced Jalapeño, its first custom inference chip built with Broadcom. The first round of news was mostly strategic narrative: reducing NVIDIA dependency, lowering costs. Everyone's immediate question was: how powerful is this chip, really? Today the community, through public filings and die analysis, put together a much more specific parameter profile. This lets you quantify what it means for compute and cost.
Chip Memory Bandwidth FP4 Compute Type
NVIDIA H100 80GB HBM3 3.35 TB/s ~4 PFLOPS General GPU (train+infer)
NVIDIA B200 192GB HBM3e 8 TB/s ~18 PFLOPS General GPU
Jalapeño 216GB HBM3E 7.1–7.4 TB/s ~10 PFLOPS Dedicated inference ASIC

Source: community analysis (Smol AI / chip analysis forums) · FP4 is integer matrix multiply equivalent

🎯 Why this matters

  • Cost structure is now estimable — Dedicated ASICs are 30-50% cheaper than general GPUs, plus OpenAI cuts out NVIDIA's margin. Inference costs could drop 40-60%. This directly determines what you'll pay for GPT and Codex going forward.
  • Power efficiency is also better — Lower power means cloud providers (Azure / AWS) can reduce instance pricing too. Every dollar you spend on APIs will buy more inference.
  • For non-developers: predictably cheaper — You don't need to understand ASIC vs GPU. Just know that API pricing will likely trend downward over the next few months. Don't lock in expensive long-term TPM contracts right now.

  1. Watch, don't act — Bookmark the OpenAI announcement page. Wait for the first API price changes after Jalapeño deployment (expected 60-90 days).
  2. If you use Codex API at scale — Don't sign lock-in contracts longer than 3 months. Wait for post-chip pricing.
  3. Rough estimate — Take your current H100-based inference unit price × 0.5. That's your likely price in 3 months. Use that as your budget ceiling now.
🔬 Deep dive — what do these numbers actually mean?
  • 216GB HBM3E memory: Enough to load a full GPT-5 or 5.5 model (quantized) on a single chip. No multi-chip sharding needed, reducing latency and engineering complexity.
  • 7.1–7.4 TB/s bandwidth: More than double H100's 3.35 TB/s, on par with B200. Bandwidth determines time-to-first-token during inference. Jalapeño's TTFT could drop from H100's ~200ms to under 100ms.
  • FP4 ~10 PFLOPS: 2.5x faster than H100. But note: this is a pure inference chip — it can't do training. OpenAI isn't planning to use it for training either.
  • vs B200: B200 has 192GB + 8 TB/s + 18 PFLOPS — it's stronger, but it's a general GPU and therefore much more expensive. Jalapeño is a "good enough at low cost" strategy.
  • Caveats: First-gen chip volume is limited. It will only handle part of the inference workload initially. High-end models may still run on GPUs. But this is OpenAI's first step away from NVIDIA dependency.

Jargon decoder:
ASIC = Application-Specific Integrated Circuit — a chip that does one thing, more power-efficient and cheaper than a general-purpose GPU.
HBM3E = High Bandwidth Memory 3 Enhanced — 10x faster than regular DDR RAM, designed to feed data to AI chips.
FP4 = 4-bit floating point — ultra-low precision compute for speed, the mainstream inference optimization today.
PFLOPS = PetaFLOPs — quadrillions of floating-point operations per second, a measure of compute power.
