🔬 Deep dive — how is this different from the GPUs you're using now?
  • vs NVIDIA H100/B200: General-purpose GPUs handle both training and inference — they're jack-of-all-trades. Jalapeño is a pure inference ASIC (Application-Specific Integrated Circuit). It does one thing (run LLMs) and does it with much better power efficiency and lower per-chip cost.
  • Key specs (community estimates): 216GB HBM3E memory provides enough capacity to hold large models (like GPT-5 class) without splitting across chips. 7.1–7.4 TB/s bandwidth means data moves between memory and compute fast enough to keep the compute units fed at high batch sizes. ~10 PFLOPS FP4 means each chip can do ~10 quadrillion 4-bit floating-point operations per second — roughly 2.5× H100's FP4 throughput.
  • Deployment plan: OpenAI says Jalapeño will initially handle a portion of inference workloads, gradually replacing NVIDIA GPUs as more chips are manufactured and deployed in data centers.
  • Why 9 months is fast: Normal chip design cycles are 2–3 years. OpenAI acquired chip startup Ona in early 2026 and paired with Broadcom's existing manufacturing flow to compress the timeline dramatically.

Jargon decoder:
ASIC = Application-Specific Integrated Circuit — a chip designed for one specific task, more efficient than a general-purpose GPU.
HBM3E = High Bandwidth Memory 3 Enhanced — ultra-fast memory specifically designed for AI accelerators.
FP4 = 4-bit floating point — a low-precision number format that lets chips run inference much faster at a small quality cost.
Tape-out = the final design is sent to manufacturing — the point where a chip design becomes a physical product.
